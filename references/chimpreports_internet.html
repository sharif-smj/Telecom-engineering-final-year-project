Uganda’s Internet users hit 13 million

Christopher Kiiza March 25, 2024
2 minutes read

As the government recognizes that digital development and innovation are priority areas that will drive prosperity and economic development in Uganda, the population is rapidly embracing digital transformation.

In January 2024, there were 13.3 million internet users in Uganda, and in the same month, Uganda’s internet penetration rate stood at 27 percent of the total population at the beginning of this year (2024), according to the Permanent Secretary for the Ministry of ICT and National Guidance, Dr. Aminah Zawede.

Zawede, who was speaking at a validation workshop on policy and regulatory framework for optical fiber infrastructure in Kampala on Monday, said that internet users in Uganda increased by 1.2 million (+10.3 percent) between January 2023 and January 2024.

As of 2023, in Uganda, 77% of the population is covered by 3G and 31% by 4G, while 53% of the country’s geography is covered by 3G and 24% by 4G. 5G technology has also been launched commercially in Uganda. 

Uganda has experienced tremendous progress in the telecommunications and ICT industries over the past few years. 

The country has a national policy and strategic framework that reviews, integrates, consolidates, and improves all the existing ICT strategies, policies, and plans into one overarching digital vision for Uganda by providing a unified direction for ICT development and an integrated ICT project implementation approach.

The principal aim of the Digital Uganda Vision is to align ICT investments in the various sectors in a manner that will improve the country’s global ICT indices for purposes of attracting investors. 

Additionally, in August 2023, Uganda launched the first ever Digital Transformation Roadmap anchored on the Uganda Vision 2040, conceptualized to strengthen the fundamentals of the economy by harnessing the abundant opportunities around the country, including opportunities within the Information and Communications Technology (ICT) sector.

The Digital Transformation Roadmap aims to strengthen the implementation of enabling policies and laws to accelerate Uganda’s Digital Revolution.

Dr. Zawede says the roadmap will provide an overarching implementation framework for a well-connected Uganda that delivers on the opportunities presented by various technologies.

The Ministry of ICT and National Guidance has implemented the National Data Transmission Backbone Infrastructure and e-Government Infrastructure Project (NBI/EGI) to connect all major towns within the country onto an optical fiber cable-based network and to connect ministries and government departments onto the e-government network.

“Through this project, the laying of over 12,000 km of optical fiber cable across the country has been achieved,” said Zawede.

GLOBAL PERSPECTIVE

In 2023, approximately 67% of the world’s population will be using the Internet. However, on the African continent, only 37% of the population was using the Internet. This is an indication that, as a continent, there is a need to improve ICT infrastructure coverage and target reaching the underserved and unserved communities.

Africa remains far behind much of the rest of the world in terms of fiber network and broadband connectivity, spectrum, and data center processing capabilities.

To bridge the gap, Dr. Zawede said, “digital infrastructure investments will require 250,000 new 4G base stations and at least 250,000 kilometers of fiber in Africa.”

“Thus, the EGEE-ICT program is vital for our region because the policy and regulatory frameworks to be developed under this program, once adopted and implemented, will create an enabling ICT environment for investment to address the ICT infrastructure gaps and improve access to affordable services. The Government of Uganda therefore gives assurance to continue to support and promote the success of the EGEE-ICT program,” she added.

OPPORTUNITIES

Having available optical fiber infrastructure across the region would have a positive effect on several other areas, such as increased cross-border trade, free movement of people, reduced cost of doing business, especially for the landlocked countries, and ultimately enhanced regional integration.

SEACOM Uganda, one of Africa’s leading converged ICT services providers, launched Africa’s first broadband submarine cable system along the continent’s eastern and southern coasts in 2009.

This marked the beginning of a new era, as the African continent was being connected to the rest of the world.

Since then, many more cables have landed at different ports along the African coastline. 

However, the challenge has been the connectivity to the land. Land-locked countries face the challenge of extending fiber connectivity on land.

Uganda, being landlocked, faces this challenge.
